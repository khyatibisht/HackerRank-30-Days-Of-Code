MARIE CURIE

(7 November 1867 – 4 July 1934)

Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less.

Marie Curie, was a pioneering physicist and chemist, who won a Nobel Peace Prize twice (1903, 1911) for her work on radioactivity. At this time, she is one of only a handful of distinguished scientists to have won two Nobel Prizes remains the only woman to have done so and was part of the Curie family legacy of five Nobel Prizes. She was also the first woman to hold Ph.D degree at a French University at that time.

Her achievements and the recognition of her work are exceedingly impressive feats considering that she lived at a time in history when women were largely excluded from higher education. She was made professor at the University of Paris - the first woman to have achieved the honour - and used her knowledge to develop mobile radiography units which were used to treat wounded French soldiers on the battlefields of World War I.

While a French citizen, Marie Skłodowska Curie (she used both surnames) never lost her sense of Polish identity. She taught her daughters the Polish language and took them on visits to Poland. She named the first chemical element that she discovered—polonium, which she isolated in 1898—after her native country. In 1920, Curie together with Claudius Regaud, founded the Curie Institute in Paris which utilised Curie's knowledge of radioactivity for the treatment of cancer, alongside surgical procedures.

Concerned more with humanitarian causes than financial rewards, Marie Curie was one of the most celebrated scientists of her time, at a time when the field was almost exclusively for men.

Life Events

1867

Born Maria Skłodowska in Warsaw, Poland. She was the youngest of five children, three older sisters and a brother.

May 1878

Maria's mother Bronisława died of tuberculosis, when Maria was ten years old.

1891

Moves to Paris and begins study of physics, chemistry, and mathematics at the University of Paris, becomes known as Marie.

1893

Earns a degree in Physics, finishes first in her class at Sorbonne and begins work at an Industrial laboratory of Professor Gabriel Lippmann

1894

Earned a degree in mathematics and finishes second in her class.

1895

Marries Pierre Curie and becomes known as Marie Curie, or Maria Skłodowska-Curie.

1897

Her daughter Irène is born and her first scientific paper is completed.

1903

Earns her Doctorate from the University of Paris supervised by Gabriel Lippmann. Received the Nobel Prize in Physics, becoming the first woman to be awarded a Nobel Prize

1904

Second daughter Eve is born

1906

Pierre dies in a road accident, leaving Marie devastated. She accepts the position as the physics department chair at the University of Paris, originally created for Pierre. First woman to become a professor at the University of Paris

1911

Marie earns second Nobel Prize (Chemistry).

1914-1919

Marie promotes and fundraises for mobile X-ray units to help give immediate attention to soldiers injured in battle.

1921

President Warren Harding presents Marie with a single gram of Radium, the National Institute of Social Sciences awards its gold medal to Marie.

1921

Toured the United States to raise funds for research on radium and second time in 1929 in equipping the Warsaw Radium Institute with radium

1922

Marie Curie becomes a member of the newly created International Commission for Intellectual Cooperation of the League of Nations

1925

Visited Poland, to participate in the ceremony that laid foundations for the Radium Institute in Warsaw

1932

Warsaw Radium Institute was opened, her sister Bronisława became its director. Now called ‘Maria Skłodowska-Curie Institute of Oncology’, the institute carries out specialized cancer research and treatment.

1934

Marie Skłodowska-Curie dies at the Sancellemoz Sanatorium in Passy, in Haute-Savoie (France), from aplastic anaemia believed to have been contracted from her long-term exposure to radiation on the 4th of July at the age of 66. She was known to carry test tubes of radium in the pocket of her lab coat.

Scientific Works/Achievements

1898 : Discovers 'Polonium' and 'Radium' - coins the term "radioactivity" to describe the emission of uranic rays. Marie and Pierre named polonium after Poland.
1898-1902 : Marie and Pierre Curie published 32 scientific papers, including one that announced that, when exposed to radium, diseased, tumour-forming cells were destroyed faster than healthy cells.
1900 : First woman-faculty member at the École Normale Supérieure.
1903 : Marie along with her husband and Henri Becquerel, earns the Nobel Prize in Physics "in recognition of the extraordinary services they have rendered by their joint researches on the radiation phenomena discovered by Professor Henri Becquerel.", the first woman to be awarded a Nobel Prize.
Davy Medal from Royal Society of London (with Pierre).
1904 : Matteucci Medal (with Pierre)
1907 : Actonian Prize for her essay "Recherches sur les Substances Radioactives."
1909 : Elliott Cresson Medal for discovery of Radium
1910 : Succeeds in isolating Radium; Defines an international standard for radioactive emissions that was eventually named for her and Pierre: the Curie.
1911 : Marie earns second Nobel Prize, but in Chemistry "in recognition of her services to the advancement of chemistry by the discovery of the elements radium and polonium, by the isolation of radium and the study of the nature and compounds of this remarkable element." She establishes the standard for radium: the curie.
1914 : She becomes director of the Red Cross Radiology Service and set up France's first military radiology centre. She directed the installation of 20 mobile radiological vehicles and another 200 radiological units at field hospitals in the first year of the war.
1915 : Produced hollow needles containing radon, to be used for sterilizing infected tissue.
1920 : Established the Curie Institute
1921 : Franklin Medal of the American Philosophical Society
1995 : Entombed in the Pantheon Paris along with her husband Pierre. Marie became the first and one of only five women to be laid to rest there. In 2017, the Panthéon hosted an exhibition to honour the 150th birthday of the pioneering scientist.

Unknown Facts


•   Marie Curie was originally ignored by the Nobel Prize Nominating Committee
      In 1903, members of the French Academy of Sciences wrote a letter to the Swedish Academy in which they nominated the collective discoveries in the field of radioactivity made by Marie and Pierre Curie, as well as their contemporary Henri Becquerel, for the Nobel Prize in Physics. Yet, in a sign of the times and its prevailing sexist attitudes, no recognition of Marie’s contributions was offered, nor was there even any mention of her name. Thankfully, a sympathetic member of the nominating committee, a professor of mathematics at Stockholm University College named Gösta Mittage-Leffler, wrote a letter to Pierre warning him of the glaring omission. Pierre, in turn, wrote the committee insisting that he and Marie be “considered together with respect to our research on radioactive bodies.” Eventually, the wording of the official nomination was amended. Later that year, thanks to a combination of her accomplishments and the combined efforts of her husband and Mittage-Leffler, Marie Curie became the first woman in history to receive the Nobel Prize.


•   She refused to cash in on her discoveries
      After discovering Radium in 1898, Marie and Pierre balked at the opportunity to pursue a patent for it and to profit from its production, despite the fact that they had barely enough money to procure the uranium slag they needed in order to extract the element. To the contrary, the Curies generously shared the isolated product of Marie's difficult labors with fellow researchers and openly distributed the secrets of the process needed for its production with interested industrial parties.


•   Her first daughter, Irene, won a Nobel Prize in Chemistry for her work with Aluminum and radiation.


•   Einstein encouraged Marie Curie during one of the worst years of her life
      Albert Einstein and Marie Curie first met in Brussels at the prestigious Solvay Conference in 1911. By this time, France had reached the peak of its rising sexism, xenophobia, and anti-semitism that defined the years preceding the First World War. Curie’s nomination to the French Academy of Sciences was rejected, and many suspected that biases against her gender and immigrant roots were to blame. Furthermore, it came to light that she had been involved in a romantic relationship with her married colleague, Paul Langevin, though he was estranged from his wife at the time. Though she had just been awarded a second Nobel Prize, the nominating committee now sought to discourage Curie from traveling to Stockholm to accept it so as to avoid a scandal. Marie Curie sank into a deep depression and retreated from the public eye. Around this time, Curie received a letter from Albert Einstein in which he described his admiration for her, as well as offered his heart-felt advice on how to handle the events as they unfolded. “I am impelled to tell you how much I have come to admire your intellect, your drive, and your honesty,” he wrote, “and that I consider myself lucky to have made your personal acquaintance...” As for the frenzy of newspaper articles attacking her, Einstein encouraged Curie “to simply not read that hogwash, but rather leave it to the reptile for whom it has been fabricated.”


•   Marie’s second daughter Ève Curie wrote the first of many biographies devoted to her famous mother, Madame Curie in 1937, which became a feature film a few years later.
